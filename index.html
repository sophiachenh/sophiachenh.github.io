Introduction¶

Music is a core part of billions of lives all around the globe. On Spotify alone, there are 615 million users and 11 million artists. Here, we are analyzing songs to see how their different features correlate with their popularity, finding out what elements contribute to their stream counts. Some questions we are trying to answer are: What features most affect popularity and stream count? And does the time of a songs release influence its popularity? These questions are important to explore so that artists know what would get them more streams, whether that be how to incorporate the specific features that enhance the songs or when they should release their music. Providing these insights to artists would help them optimize their reach and impact, revolutionizing the music industry.

Statistical Method 1: Independent T-test

We are curious if different types of qualities of the tracks indicate the popularity of the song. Here, we are focusing on the mode. Since, there are two categories for mode (Major or Minor) we are using a t-test. Popularity can be seen through their rankings on spotify charts or streams, but we chose streams because streams from a song are independent from another song. We will be performing an independent t-test to see if there is statistically significant difference in the mean number of streams between songs in major and songs in minor.

H0: There isn't a difference between the mean number of streams between major and minor modes.

HA: There is a difference between the mean number of streams between major and minor modes.

Our alpha value here is 0.05.

# performming T-test
t, p_value = stats.ttest_ind(major, minor)
print("p-value:", p_value)

After performing the t-test, and comparing the p-value to the alpha value we will fail to reject H0, so there isn't enough evidence to conclude that there is a difference in the number of streams between the major and minor modes.

Statistical Method 2: ANOVA

Here, we will be conducting an analysis on whether is a statistically significant difference in popularity with respects to the keys of a song which will also be accompanied by visuals comparing the magnitude in streams between the keys.

H0: The means of the streams of the keys are equal.

Ha: The means of the streams of the keys are unequal.

With a p-value of 0.6257, we can safely conclude there is no difference in popularity between songs of different keys.

Primary Analysis¶

For our primary analysis, we began by creating a new column called 'pop_level' which would be assigned string values 'super' or 'moderate' based on whether the stream count for each song was either higher or lower than the median of the streams of all the songs in the dataset.

We subsequently began assigning the columns of 'danceability%', 'energy%', 'acousticness_%' values of 1,2,3 based on whether they fell into the respective ranges of 0-30, 31-70, or 71-100.

Afterwhich we used a decision tree to fit the song attribute columns against the stream count as well as calculate both the precision, accuracy, recall, and f1 score.

In [ ]:
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from sklearn.tree import DecisionTreeClassifier, plot_tree
from sklearn.metrics import accuracy_score, precision_score, recall_score, f1_score


charts_df = pd.read_csv('spotify_2023_clean.csv')
charts_df['key'] = charts_df['key'].astype(str)

# Song's musical attributes
song_attr = ['danceability_%', 'energy_%', 'acousticness_%']

median_value = charts_df['streams'].median()
charts_df['pop_level'] = charts_df['streams'].apply(lambda x: 'super' if x > median_value else 'moderate')
for i in song_attr:
  charts_df[i] = charts_df[i].apply(lambda x: 1 if (x >= 0 and x <= 30) else 2 if (x >= 31 and x <= 70) else 3)

X = charts_df[song_attr]
y = charts_df['pop_level']

# Create and train the decision tree classifier
model = DecisionTreeClassifier()
model.fit(X, y)

prediction = model.predict(X)

# Calculating accuracy, precision, recall, and f1 score
accuracy = accuracy_score(y, prediction)
precision = precision_score(y, prediction, average='weighted')
recall = recall_score(y, prediction, average='weighted')
f1 = f1_score(y, prediction, average='weighted')

print("accuracy: ", accuracy)
print("precision: ", precision)
print("recall: ", recall)
print("f1 score: ", f1)

accuracy:  0.5798319327731093
precision:  0.5806520653492188
recall:  0.5798319327731093
f1 score:  0.5787610619469027

The accuracy, precision, recall, and f1 score of the decision tree classification model were all around 58%, meaning that it moderately performed for all.

Insights and Conclusions¶

We've found that although songs that have major ("happier") keys or older release dates seemingly perform slightly better, further hypothesis testing using an independent t-test and ANOVA test revealed that mode, key, and release date do not have a statistically significant effect on a song's popularity on Spotify. Our decision tree also showed what audio features contribute to a song's popularity - we found that the highest performing songs have low danceability, low energy, and high acousticness, while the lowest performing songs have high danceability, low energy, and low acousticness, or high energy and high acousticness. These results show us that there are many factors that go into a song's popularity, and that certain traits/audio features aren't necessarily indicative of how well a song will perform on Spotify.
